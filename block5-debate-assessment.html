The EMH Challenge to Technical Analysis

What is EMH?

The Efficient Market Hypothesis (Eugene Fama, 1970) states that asset prices fully reflect all available information, making it impossible to consistently "beat the market."

Three Forms

  • Weak Form: Prices reflect all past trading data → TA is useless
  • Semi-Strong: Prices reflect all public info → FA is also useless
  • Strong: Prices reflect everything, even insider info → nobody can beat the market
KEY POINTS:
  • EMH was formalized by Eugene Fama in 1970 -- Nobel Prize winner
  • The pyramid shows increasing levels of market efficiency
  • Each form has different implications for different types of analysis
  • The weak form is the most relevant to our discussion of TA
SAY: "The Efficient Market Hypothesis is the strongest academic argument against Technical Analysis. It comes in three forms, shown in this pyramid. The weak form -- the base -- is the one that directly challenges TA, because it says that all past price information is already baked into the current price."

Weak Form EMH vs Technical Analysis

The Weak Form Argument

  • Random Walk Theory: Price changes are random and independent -- tomorrow's price cannot be predicted from today's
  • Past prices, volume, and trading data are already reflected in current prices
  • Any patterns found in historical data are coincidental, not predictive
  • If patterns worked, arbitrageurs would exploit them until they disappear
"A blindfolded monkey throwing darts at a newspaper's financial pages could select a portfolio that would do just as well as one carefully selected by experts." -- Burton Malkiel, A Random Walk Down Wall Street (1973)

The TA Counter-Argument

  • Markets are NOT perfectly efficient -- information takes time to disseminate and process
  • Human behavior is not random -- fear, greed, and herding create repeatable patterns
  • Self-fulfilling prophecy: When millions of traders watch the same levels, those levels matter
  • Institutional order flow creates detectable price impact patterns
  • Empirical evidence: Some TA strategies show statistically significant returns
Key insight: Markets can be "mostly efficient" while still leaving exploitable inefficiencies
KEY POINTS:
  • The random walk theory is the mathematical foundation of weak-form EMH
  • Malkiel's monkey quote is one of the most famous in finance
  • TA practitioners argue markets are efficient but not perfectly so
  • The self-fulfilling prophecy argument is particularly interesting
SAY: "The weak form of EMH says that since all past price data is already in the current price, looking at charts is pointless -- prices follow a random walk. But TA practitioners fight back. They argue that markets are not perfectly efficient, that human psychology creates repeatable patterns, and that when enough traders watch the same support level, that level becomes real."

Behavioral Finance: Why TA Works Despite EMH

Behavioral finance explains market inefficiencies through systematic cognitive biases that create exploitable patterns.

Cognitive Biases

  • Anchoring: Traders fixate on round numbers and past prices (support/resistance)
  • Overconfidence: Traders overreact to new information, creating overshooting
  • Confirmation Bias: Seeing patterns that confirm existing beliefs

Herd Behavior

  • Herding: Traders follow the crowd, amplifying trends and creating momentum
  • FOMO: Fear of missing out drives buying at peaks
  • Panic Selling: Fear drives selling at bottoms -- exactly when TA signals "oversold"

Market Structure

  • Self-Fulfilling Prophecy: If enough traders act on the same signal, it becomes real
  • Information Asymmetry: Not everyone gets information at the same time
  • Limits to Arbitrage: Even when mispricings exist, correcting them is costly and risky
Prospect Theory (Kahneman & Tversky, 1979): People feel losses ~2x more than equivalent gains. This asymmetry creates predictable behaviors at support/resistance levels -- exactly what TA exploits.
KEY POINTS:
  • Behavioral finance bridges the gap between EMH and TA
  • Kahneman won the Nobel Prize for Prospect Theory -- losses hurt 2x more than gains feel good
  • The self-fulfilling prophecy is the strongest argument: if millions watch the 200-day MA, it matters
  • EMH assumes rational actors, but humans are not rational
SAY: "Behavioral finance is TA's best defense against EMH. EMH assumes rational investors, but we know from Kahneman and Tversky that humans are systematically irrational. We anchor on round numbers -- that's why support and resistance work. We herd -- that's why trends persist. And when enough people watch the same moving average, it becomes a self-fulfilling prophecy. TA doesn't predict the future -- it predicts human behavior."

Advantages of Technical Analysis

Visual & Intuitive

Charts tell a story at a glance. Even beginners can spot basic trends and patterns without deep financial expertise.

Any Timeframe

Works on 1-minute charts for day traders and monthly charts for long-term investors. Same principles, any scale.

Universal Application

Applies to stocks, forex, commodities, crypto, bonds -- any market with price and volume data.

Entry & Exit Points

Provides specific, actionable levels for when to buy, sell, and place stop-losses. Removes emotional guessing.

Risk Management

Stop-loss levels, position sizing, and risk/reward ratios are built into TA methodology. Protects capital systematically.

Complements FA

FA tells you what to buy (undervalued stocks). TA tells you when to buy (optimal entry timing). Use both.

KEY POINTS:
  • TA is visual -- you can see the story in the chart without reading financial statements
  • Works on any timeframe and any market -- truly universal
  • Provides specific, actionable trading levels -- not vague advice
  • The best approach combines FA (what to buy) with TA (when to buy)

Limitations of Technical Analysis

Subjective Interpretation

Two analysts can look at the same chart and draw different conclusions. Pattern identification is more art than science.

Self-Fulfilling Prophecy

Signals may work only because everyone acts on them -- not because they reflect true market dynamics. Circular reasoning risk.

No Guaranteed Profits

No TA strategy works 100% of the time. Even the best signals fail regularly. TA is about probabilities, not certainties.

Ignores Fundamentals

Pure TA ignores earnings, revenue, management quality, and industry dynamics. A "bullish chart" can belong to a bankrupt company.

Requires Experience

Effective TA requires years of practice. Beginners often misidentify patterns, leading to costly mistakes and overconfidence.

Past ≠ Future

Historical patterns may not repeat. Black swan events (pandemics, wars, regulatory changes) can invalidate all technical signals instantly.

KEY POINTS:
  • Subjectivity is TA's biggest weakness -- it's more art than science
  • The self-fulfilling prophecy cuts both ways -- is it real analysis or crowd behavior?
  • TA works in probabilities, not certainties -- even the best signals fail 30-40% of the time
  • Black swan events can wipe out all technical signals
SAY: "We must be honest about TA's limitations. It's subjective -- two people can see different things in the same chart. It ignores fundamentals entirely, so a beautiful bullish chart could belong to a company about to go bankrupt. And most importantly, past patterns do not guarantee future results. Black swan events like COVID-19 or the 2008 financial crisis made every chart worthless overnight."

Block 5 Summary: A Balanced View

1
EMH challenges TA -- but markets are not perfectly efficient. Behavioral biases create exploitable patterns.
2
