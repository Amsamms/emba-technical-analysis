Two Schools of Thought

Fundamental Analysis

"What is this asset worth?"

Value Investing Long-Term

Studies financial statements, earnings, economic indicators, and management quality to determine intrinsic value.

Champions: Warren Buffett, Benjamin Graham

Technical Analysis

"When should I act?"

Price Action Timing

Studies price charts, volume, patterns, and indicators to identify optimal entry/exit points.

Champions: Charles Dow, John Murphy, Martin Pring

Both are valid approaches. The debate is not which is "right" — it is which is more useful for your specific goal.

KEY POINTS:
  • FA asks "what is this worth?" — TA asks "when should I act?"
  • FA focuses on intrinsic value, TA on price action and timing
  • Both approaches are valid — the right choice depends on your goal
SAY: "There are two main schools of thought in market analysis. Fundamental Analysis, championed by Buffett and Graham, asks what an asset is truly worth by studying financials and earnings. Technical Analysis, rooted in the work of Charles Dow, asks when you should act by studying price and volume. Neither is inherently right or wrong — they serve different purposes."

TA vs FA: Data Sources

Dimension Fundamental Analysis Technical Analysis
Primary Data Financial statements, earnings reports, balance sheets Price charts, volume data, candlestick patterns
Economic Data GDP, interest rates, inflation, unemployment Not directly used — assumed to be "in the price"
Company Info Management quality, competitive advantage, moat Not considered — price tells the story
Market Data P/E ratios, book value, dividend yield Moving averages, RSI, MACD, Bollinger Bands
Sentiment Analyst reports, news, industry trends Volume spikes, price patterns, momentum indicators
Output Intrinsic value estimate (fair price) Buy/sell signals (entry/exit timing)
KEY POINTS:
  • FA uses financial statements, economic data, company information
  • TA uses price charts, volume data, and mathematical indicators
  • FA outputs a fair value estimate; TA outputs buy/sell signals
  • TA assumes all fundamental data is already reflected in the price

Can They Work Together?

The Combined Approach

  1. FA Screen: Find fundamentally strong companies (good earnings, low debt, competitive moat)
  2. TA Timing: Wait for a technical buy signal (breakout, support bounce, bullish pattern)
  3. TA Exit: Use stop-losses and resistance levels to manage the trade

Example: FA tells you Apple is undervalued at $170. TA tells you to buy when it bounces off the 200-day moving average with rising volume. FA says WHAT, TA says WHEN.

KEY POINTS:
  • FA and TA overlap in areas like volume analysis, risk management, and trend identification
  • The combined approach: FA screens for opportunities, TA times the entry
  • Many successful professional traders use both approaches

The most effective approach combines both: use FA to find fundamentally strong companies, then use TA to time your entry and exit. Principle 1: Market Discounts Everything

What This Means

Every piece of information — economic data, company earnings, news, even insider knowledge — is already reflected in the current market price.

Implication for Traders

You do not need to study financials or wait for news. The price IS the news. Just read the chart.

Academic Connection

This mirrors the semi-strong form of EMH — but TA practitioners add: "and price patterns reveal where the market is heading next."

KEY POINTS:
  • All information flows into the market and is reflected in the price
  • Economic data, financials, news, sentiment — all are "priced in"
  • This is why TA practitioners focus only on price and volume
  • Ironically, this principle aligns with semi-strong EMH
SAY: "The first principle is that the market discounts everything. Think of the price as a funnel — all information flows in from the top: economic data, company financials, news, even insider activity. By the time it reaches the market, it is all reflected in one number: the price. This is why technical analysts focus solely on price and volume — they believe the chart already contains all the information you need."

Principle 2: Prices Move in Trends

The Trend is Your Friend

Prices do not move randomly. They form trends — sustained directional movements driven by supply and demand imbalances.

Three Types of Trends

  • Uptrend: Higher Highs (HH) + Higher Lows (HL)
  • Downtrend: Lower Highs (LH) + Lower Lows (LL)
  • Sideways: Price oscillates between support and resistance

Newton's First Law of Markets: A trend in motion tends to stay in motion until acted upon by an external force (reversal signal).

KEY POINTS:
  • Prices move in trends, not randomly
  • Uptrend = Higher Highs + Higher Lows
  • Downtrend = Lower Highs + Lower Lows
  • "The trend is your friend" — one of the most famous trading maxims
  • Trends persist until a clear reversal signal appears
SAY: "The second principle is that prices move in trends. Look at this uptrend diagram — you can see a clear pattern of Higher Highs and Higher Lows. This is not random movement; it reflects a sustained buying pressure. There is a famous saying in trading: the trend is your friend. The idea is simple — once a trend is established, it tends to continue until something forces a reversal. Think of it as Newton's first law applied to markets."

TRANSITION: "And the third principle explains why patterns keep appearing in markets."

Principle 3: History Repeats Itself

Why It Repeats

Human psychology does not change. Fear, greed, hope, and panic drive the same patterns generation after generation.

The Evidence

The same candlestick patterns Homma identified in the 1700s still work in today's AI-driven crypto markets. Same emotions, same patterns.

Behavioral Finance Link

Chapter 12 of our course explains this: cognitive biases (anchoring, herding, loss aversion) create predictable, repeating price patterns.

KEY POINTS:
  • Market patterns repeat because human psychology does not change
  • Fear, greed, and herding behavior create the same patterns over centuries
  • This connects to Behavioral Finance (Chapter 12) — cognitive biases drive market cycles
  • The cycle: Optimism → Euphoria → Panic → Recovery → repeat
SAY: "The third principle is that history repeats itself — and this is perhaps the most powerful idea in TA. Look at this market cycle: optimism leads to euphoria, which leads to panic, then recovery, and the cycle starts again. Why? Because human psychology does not change. The same fear and greed that drove Japanese rice traders in the 1700s drives crypto traders today. This connects directly to our Chapter 12 on Behavioral Finance — cognitive biases like herding and loss aversion create these predictable patterns."

Block 2 — Key Takeaways

1
FA and TA are complementary, not competing.

FA answers WHAT to buy (intrinsic value), TA answers WHEN to buy (timing). The best approach often combines both.

2
Three principles form the foundation of all TA:

Market discounts everything (price contains all info), prices move in trends (not randomly), and history repeats (psychology is constant).

3
TA excels in short-to-medium term trading across all market conditions.

Whether markets are trending, ranging, or volatile, TA provides actionable signals for timing entries and exits.
