Reversal vs. Continuation Patterns

Reversal Patterns

Signal that the current trend is about to change direction

  • Head & Shoulders — bearish reversal
  • Inverse H&S — bullish reversal
  • Double Top — bearish reversal
  • Double Bottom — bullish reversal

Appear at the END of a trend

Continuation Patterns

Signal a temporary pause before the trend resumes

  • Ascending Triangle — bullish
  • Descending Triangle — bearish
  • Symmetrical Triangle — neutral (wait for direction)
  • Flags & Pennants — trend continuation

Appear DURING a trend (pause & continue)

Key Rule: Always confirm patterns with volume and other indicators. No pattern is 100% reliable on its own.

KEY POINTS:
  • Two categories: reversal (trend change) and continuation (pause then resume)
  • Reversal patterns form at the end of trends
  • Continuation patterns form during trends as temporary consolidations
  • Always confirm with volume
SAY: "Chart patterns fall into two categories. Reversal patterns tell us the trend is about to flip — like a Head and Shoulders at the top of an uptrend. Continuation patterns tell us the trend is just taking a breather — like a flag or triangle before the next leg. Let's look at each one."
TIMING: 30-40 seconds
TRANSITION: "The most famous reversal pattern is the Head and Shoulders."

Head & Shoulders

Bearish Reversal — One of the most reliable patterns in technical analysis

Anatomy

  1. Left Shoulder — peak, then pullback to neckline
  2. Head — higher peak, then pullback to neckline
  3. Right Shoulder — lower peak (can't reach head height)
  4. Neckline — support connecting the two troughs

Trading Rules

  • Entry: When price breaks below neckline
  • Target: Distance from head to neckline, projected downward
  • Volume: Should decline from left to right shoulder
  • Reliability: High — ~70% success rate
KEY POINTS:
  • H&S is a bearish reversal pattern forming after an uptrend
  • Three peaks: left shoulder, head (highest), right shoulder (lower than head)
  • Neckline is the key level — break below it confirms the pattern
  • Price target = head-to-neckline distance projected below neckline
SAY: "The Head and Shoulders is considered the most reliable reversal pattern. It forms three peaks — the middle one highest. When price breaks below the neckline, it often falls by the same distance as the head-to-neckline height. Volume typically decreases from left shoulder to right shoulder, showing weakening buying pressure."
TIMING: 40-50 seconds
TRANSITION: "Another common reversal pattern is the Double Top and Double Bottom."

Double Top & Double Bottom

Double Top Bearish

Double Bottom Bullish

Double Top: Price hits resistance twice, fails to break through. Break below support confirms bearish reversal. Looks like the letter "M".

Double Bottom: Price hits support twice, holds both times. Break above resistance confirms bullish reversal. Looks like the letter "W".

KEY POINTS:
  • Double Top = "M" shape, bearish. Price fails to break resistance twice.
  • Double Bottom = "W" shape, bullish. Price holds support twice.
  • Confirmation comes when the middle trough (top) or middle peak (bottom) is broken
SAY: "Double tops and bottoms are easy to recognize. A double top looks like the letter M — price hits the same ceiling twice and falls. A double bottom looks like a W — price bounces off the same floor twice and rises. The pattern is confirmed when price breaks through the middle level."
TIMING: 35-45 seconds
TRANSITION: "Now let's look at triangle patterns — these are continuation patterns."

Triangle Patterns

Continuation Patterns — Price consolidates into a narrowing range before breaking out

Ascending Triangle

Descending Triangle

Symmetrical Triangle

Key: Triangles compress volatility. The breakout direction determines the trade. Volume typically decreases inside the triangle and spikes on the breakout.

KEY POINTS:
  • Ascending: flat top + rising bottom = bullish bias
  • Descending: flat bottom + falling top = bearish bias
  • Symmetrical: converging lines = wait for direction
  • All three: volume contracts inside, expands on breakout
SAY: "Triangles show price compressing into a tighter and tighter range. An ascending triangle has a flat top with rising lows — it usually breaks up. A descending triangle has a flat bottom with falling highs — it usually breaks down. A symmetrical triangle could go either way — you have to wait for the breakout direction."
TIMING: 40-50 seconds
TRANSITION: "Two more continuation patterns: flags and pennants."

Flags & Pennants

Continuation Patterns — Brief pauses in strong trends

Bull Flag

  • Sharp rally (flagpole)
  • Slight downward channel (flag)
  • Breakout resumes the uptrend
  • Target = flagpole height added to breakout point

Pennant

  • Sharp rally (flagpole)
  • Converging lines (pennant shape)
  • Breakout resumes the uptrend
  • Similar to flag but with converging boundaries
KEY POINTS:
  • Flags and pennants form after sharp moves (the flagpole)
  • Flag = parallel channel consolidation, Pennant = converging consolidation
  • Both are short-term continuation patterns
  • Price target = flagpole height projected from breakout
SAY: "Flags and pennants form after a sharp price move. The sharp move is the flagpole. Then price consolidates briefly — in a channel for flags, or converging lines for pennants. The breakout continues the original trend. They're some of the most reliable short-term patterns."

TRANSITION: "Let's start with the most fundamental indicator: Moving Averages."

Moving Averages (MA)

Trend Following — Smooth out price noise to reveal the underlying trend

SMA vs. EMA

  • SMA (Simple) — equal weight to all periods
  • EMA (Exponential) — more weight to recent prices
  • EMA reacts faster to new price changes

Crossover Signals

  • Golden Cross: Short MA crosses ABOVE long MA = Bullish
  • Death Cross: Short MA crosses BELOW long MA = Bearish

Common pairs: 20/50, 50/200 day

KEY POINTS:
  • Moving averages smooth price data to show trend direction
  • SMA = simple average, EMA = weighted toward recent prices
  • Golden Cross (bullish) and Death Cross (bearish) are key signals
  • 50/200 day crossovers are watched by institutional investors
SAY: "Moving averages are the most widely used indicator. They smooth out price noise to show you the trend. The chart shows a 20-day and 50-day SMA plus a 20-day EMA. When the short-term average crosses above the long-term one, that's a Golden Cross — a bullish signal. The opposite is a Death Cross."
TIMING: 40-50 seconds
TRANSITION: "Next, let's look at RSI — a momentum oscillator."

RSI — Relative Strength Index

Momentum Oscillator — Measures speed and magnitude of price changes (0-100 scale)

Overbought (> 70)

  • Price may be too high, too fast
  • Potential pullback or reversal ahead
  • Not an automatic sell signal!

Oversold (< 30)

  • Price may be too low, too fast
  • Potential bounce or reversal ahead
  • Look for confirmation before buying

Divergence

  • Price makes new high but RSI doesn't = bearish divergence
  • Price makes new low but RSI doesn't = bullish divergence
  • Powerful early warning signal
KEY POINTS:
  • RSI oscillates between 0-100, default period = 14
  • Above 70 = overbought, Below 30 = oversold
  • Divergence between price and RSI is a powerful reversal signal
  • RSI can stay overbought/oversold for extended periods in strong trends
SAY: "RSI measures momentum on a 0 to 100 scale. Above 70 means the stock might be overbought — it's run up too fast. Below 30 means it might be oversold. But the real power is in divergence: when price makes a new high but RSI makes a lower high, it's warning you that momentum is fading."
TIMING: 40-50 seconds
TRANSITION: "Another powerful momentum indicator is MACD."

MACD — Moving Average Convergence Divergence

Trend + Momentum — Combines trend following with momentum analysis

Three Components

  1. MACD Line — 12 EMA minus 26 EMA
  2. Signal Line — 9 EMA of MACD line
  3. Histogram — difference between MACD and Signal

Trading Signals

  • Bullish: MACD crosses ABOVE signal line
  • Bearish: MACD crosses BELOW signal line
  • Histogram grows = momentum increasing
  • Histogram shrinks = momentum weakening

Centerline: MACD above zero = bullish trend, below zero = bearish trend

KEY POINTS:
  • MACD = 12 EMA - 26 EMA, Signal = 9 EMA of MACD
  • Crossover of MACD and signal line generates buy/sell signals
  • Histogram shows momentum strength visually
  • Above/below zero line indicates overall trend direction
SAY: "MACD is one of the most versatile indicators. It shows both trend direction and momentum in one chart. The MACD line is the difference between a fast and slow moving average. When it crosses above the signal line, that's a buy signal. The histogram makes these crossovers easy to spot — green bars growing means bullish momentum."
TIMING: 40-50 seconds
TRANSITION: "Now let's look at Bollinger Bands, which measure volatility."

Bollinger Bands

Volatility Indicator — Bands expand and contract based on price volatility

Construction

  • Middle: 20-day SMA
  • Upper: SMA + 2 standard deviations
  • Lower: SMA - 2 standard deviations

Key Concepts

  • Squeeze: Bands narrow = low volatility, breakout coming
  • Expansion: Bands widen = high volatility move in progress
  • Mean Reversion: Price tends to return to the middle band

Rule of thumb: ~95% of price action stays within the bands. Price touching the upper band doesn't automatically mean "sell".

KEY POINTS:
  • Bollinger Bands = SMA +/- 2 standard deviations
  • Squeeze = low volatility, often precedes a big move
  • Band touches are not automatic signals — context matters
  • Mean reversion: price tends to return to the middle band
SAY: "Bollinger Bands wrap around price using standard deviation. When the bands squeeze together, it means volatility is low — and a big move is likely coming. When price touches the upper band, it doesn't mean sell — in a strong uptrend, price can ride the upper band for a long time. The key concept is mean reversion: price tends to come back to the middle."
TIMING: 40-50 seconds
TRANSITION: "Finally, let's look at the most overlooked indicator: volume."

Volume Analysis

Confirmation Tool — Volume confirms price moves and reveals conviction

Volume Confirms Price

  • Price up + Volume up = strong move, likely to continue
  • Price up + Volume down = weak rally, may reverse
  • Price down + Volume up = strong selling, watch out
  • Price down + Volume down = weak decline, may bounce

Key Rules

  • Volume should increase in the direction of the trend
  • Volume spikes at breakouts confirm the move
  • Declining volume in a trend = exhaustion warning

"Volume precedes price" — changes in volume often signal price moves before they happen

KEY POINTS:
  • Volume = number of shares traded, shows conviction behind price moves
  • Price move + high volume = conviction, price move + low volume = suspect
  • Volume spikes at breakouts confirm the breakout is real
  • "Volume precedes price" — watch for volume changes as early warnings
SAY: "Volume is the most underrated indicator. It tells you HOW MUCH conviction is behind a price move. A breakout on high volume? That's real. A breakout on low volume? That's probably going to fail. Always look at volume alongside price — never in isolation. As the saying goes, volume precedes price."
