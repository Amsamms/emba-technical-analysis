Introduction

What is Technical Analysis?

Technical Analysis is the study of past market data — primarily price and volume — to forecast future price movements.

Who Uses It?

Day Traders Hedge Funds Retail Investors Algorithmic Systems Investment Banks

The Key Distinction

TA answers WHEN to trade — timing entries and exits.
FA answers WHAT to trade — finding undervalued assets.
The best traders use both together.

“The market is a barometer, not a thermometer. It tells you what is going to happen, not what has already happened.” — Charles Dow, founder of Dow Jones & Co.

Why It Matters

  • Works across all asset classes — stocks, forex, crypto, commodities
  • Provides objective signals in emotional markets
  • The backbone of algorithmic trading (70%+ of volume)
  • Self-fulfilling — millions watch the same levels
Chapter I · Origins

The Ancient Roots

Before charts existed, humans were already reading markets

~2000 BC
B

Babylonian Traders

Clay tablets from ancient Mesopotamia record commodity prices for grain, wool, and sesame over time. Merchants tracked fluctuations to time their trades — the earliest known market data analysis.

4,000 years before computers, traders were already looking for patterns.

1636–37
T

Dutch Tulip Mania

The first documented speculative bubble. Tulip bulb prices rose 20× in months, then crashed 99%. The classic pattern emerged: euphoria → mania → panic → crash.

The boom-bust cycle that would repeat throughout all of financial history.

1688
V

Joseph de la Vega

Wrote “Confusion de Confusiones” — the first book describing stock exchange mechanics at the Amsterdam Exchange. Documented manipulation, speculation, and emotional price cycles.

The first written analysis of market psychology.

Key Insight
Long before any formal theory, humans understood that markets move in patterns driven by psychology — greed, fear, and herd behavior. This is the foundation that all of Technical Analysis is built upon.
Chapter II · The Father of TA

Munehisa Homma & The Birth of Candlesticks

Dojima Rice Exchange, Osaka, Japan — 1700s

The “God of Markets”

Munehisa Homma (1724–1803) was a rice trader from Sakata who amassed a fortune equivalent to $10 billion in today’s money. He didn't just trade — he invented a visual language for reading markets.

His candlestick charts encode four data points (open, high, low, close) into a single visual element, making price action instantly readable.

Sakata’s Five Methods

  • San-zan (Three Mountains) — triple top reversal
  • San-sen (Three Rivers) — triple bottom reversal
  • San-ku (Three Gaps) — exhaustion signals
  • San-pei (Three Soldiers) — continuation strength
  • San-pou (Three Methods) — rest, then continuation
“When all are bearish, there is cause for prices to rise. When all are bullish, there is cause for prices to fall.” — Munehisa Homma, “The Fountain of Gold” (c. 1755)

250 Years Later — Still #1

  • Visual clarity — instantly shows bullish vs bearish sentiment
  • Psychology encoded — each candle tells a buyer/seller battle
  • Universal — stocks, forex, crypto, commodities
  • Patterns persist — Doji, Hammer, Engulfing, Morning Star
Did You Know?
Homma’s candlestick method remained a Japanese secret for nearly 200 years until Steve Nison introduced it to the Western world in 1991.
Chapter III · The Architect

Charles Dow & Modern Technical Analysis

Wall Street, 1880s–1900s — The framework that built an industry

The Man Behind the Index

Charles Henry Dow (1851–1902) co-founded Dow Jones & Company (1882), launched the Wall Street Journal, and created the Dow Jones Industrial Average (1896) — originally just 12 stocks.

Remarkably, Dow never wrote a book. His entire theory comes from 255 editorials in the WSJ. After his death, William Hamilton and Robert Rhea compiled these into Dow Theory.

“The market is a barometer, not a thermometer.” — Charles Dow, Wall Street Journal editorial

The Six Tenets of Dow Theory

  1. The market discounts everything — all known information is already in the price
  2. Three types of trends — primary (months–years), secondary (weeks–months), minor (days)
  3. Primary trends have three phases — accumulation, public participation, distribution
  4. Indices must confirm each other — Industrial and Transport must agree
  5. Volume confirms the trend — volume rises in the trend’s direction
  6. Trends persist until clear reversal — the trend is your friend
Legacy
Every modern TA concept — trend following, support/resistance, volume analysis, market phases — traces back to these six tenets. Dow Theory is the DNA of Technical Analysis.
Chapter IV · The Visionaries

The Early Pioneers

1900s–1940s — Three men who shaped how we read markets

W

Richard Wyckoff

1873–1934 · The Tape Reader

Started as a stock runner at age 15 on Wall Street. Created the Wyckoff Method — a systematic approach to reading market structure.

  • Introduced the “Composite Operator” concept — smart money that moves markets
  • Pioneered volume spread analysis
  • 4 market phases: accumulation, markup, distribution, markdown

Still taught in CMT exams and used by institutions today.

G

W.D. Gann

1878–1955 · The Geometric Mystic

The most controversial figure in TA. Believed markets follow geometric and mathematical laws rooted in natural cycles.

  • Gann Angles — geometric trendlines for support/resistance
  • Square of Nine — mathematical price/time projections
  • Reportedly turned $130 into $12,000 in 30 days (verified 1909)

Methods mix geometry and numerology. Debated but still followed.

E

R.N. Elliott

1871–1948 · The Wave Theorist

A retired accountant who studied 75 years of market data and discovered repetitive wave patterns.

  • Published “The Wave Principle” (1938)
  • Markets move in 5 impulse + 3 corrective waves
  • Waves follow Fibonacci ratios (1.618, 0.618)
  • Theory is fractal — applies across all timeframes

Fibonacci retracements are standard on every trading platform.

Block II · Two Schools

Two Lenses on the Same Market

Different questions, different tools, complementary answers

Fundamental Analysis

Intrinsic Value

Evaluates a security by studying financial statements, economic indicators, and industry conditions to estimate what it is really worth.

Key Question

“Is this stock undervalued or overvalued?”

Technical Analysis

Price & Volume

Studies historical price movements, volume, and chart patterns to forecast where the market is most likely to go next.

Key Question

“What will the price do next?”

Bottom line
Fundamental analysis asks WHAT to buy. Technical analysis answers WHEN to buy. The most disciplined investors use both.

Block II · The Bedrock

The Three Core Principles of Technical Analysis

Every indicator, pattern, and strategy rests on these three ideas

Principle I

The Market Discounts Everything

Fundamentals, politics, psychology, earnings surprises — every known and even suspected factor is already embedded in the current price.

Studying price means studying the sum of all information.

Principle II

Prices Move in Trends

Markets do not wander randomly — they move in identifiable uptrends, downtrends, and sideways ranges that persist until a reversal confirms.

“A trend in motion tends to stay in motion.”

Principle III

History Repeats Itself

Human psychology — fear and greed — is constant. The same chart patterns recur because the emotions driving them never change.

Patterns work because people are consistent.

Core Assumption
If any one of these three fails, Technical Analysis collapses. All charting, all indicators, all pattern recognition — every tool in the toolbox — inherits these three beliefs.

Block III · Dow’s Framework

The Three Trends Every Market Moves In

Charles Dow’s ocean metaphor — the tide, the waves, and the ripples

I
The Tide

Primary Trend

The long-term direction, lasting months to years. Bull market or bear market. This is what defines an era.

II
The Waves

Secondary Trend

Medium-term movements against the primary trend. Corrections and retracements, lasting weeks to a few months.

III
The Ripples

Minor Trend

Day-to-day fluctuations — noise at the surface. Short-lived and often misleading when viewed in isolation.

Dow's insight (1900)
Markets are not random. They move in identifiable, nested trends of different time scales. Trade with the tide, surf the waves, ignore the ripples.
Block III · The Tools of Sight

Three Ways to See Price Action

Line, Bar, Candlestick — each tells a different layer of the story

Line Chart

Plots only the closing price over time. Filters intraday noise — the day’s final consensus of value.

Best for: long-term trend visualization

Bar Chart (OHLC)

Encodes four points per bar: Open (left tick), High (top), Low (bottom), Close (right tick). Reveals intraday volatility.

Best for: Western-style traders

Candlestick Chart

Same OHLC data, but the body colour instantly shows who won the period — buyers (green) or sellers (red).

Best for: reading market psychology

Block III · Anatomy of a Candle

The Language Japanese Rice Traders Invented

Munehisa Homma, Dojima Rice Exchange, 1700s — brought West by Steve Nison in the 1990s

Four data points, one glance

  • Real body — thick rectangle between open and close
  • Upper wick — thin line showing the session high
  • Lower wick — thin line showing the session low
  • Green/white body — close above open · buyers won
  • Red/black body — close below open · sellers won
Homma realised that while supply and demand set price, the emotions of traders — fear and greed — drove the fluctuations. Legend credits him with 100 consecutive winning trades. — Dojima Rice Exchange, Osaka
Block III · Bullish Reversals

When the Tide Turns Upward

Three candle formations that signal a downtrend is exhausting

The Hammer

Small body near the top with a long lower wick. Sellers drove price down but buyers fought back and won the session.

Bullish Engulfing

A large green candle completely swallows the prior red candle. Visual definition of buyers overwhelming sellers.

Morning Star

Three candles: big red → indecision dot → big green. The classic rebirth pattern — the moment before dawn.

Block III · Bearish Reversals

When the Tide Turns Downward

Three formations that appear at the peak — plus the great neutral pattern

The Hanging Man

Same shape as a Hammer but at the top of an uptrend. A warning: buyers are losing grip, sellers have arrived.

Bearish Engulfing

A large red candle swallows a small green one — the visual signature of panic selling taking over.

Evening Star

Big green → indecision → big red. The sun setting on the uptrend — the bearish twin of the Morning Star.

The Doji — Market Indecision

Open and close are virtually identical. Neither side won the session. Often a warning that the current trend is pausing — and may be about to reverse.

Block III · The Battlefield

Support & Resistance — The Invisible Lines

Price levels where the war between buyers and sellers is fought, again and again

Support — the demand floor

A price level where buying interest is strong enough to prevent further decline. As price falls, it looks cheap — demand revives, supply dries up, and the fall halts.

Resistance — the supply ceiling

A price level where selling pressure overwhelms buyers and stops the advance. As price rises, holders take profits and new buyers hesitate.

Price bounces between the floor and the ceiling — each retest strengthens the level’s significance.

Role Reversal

Once a key level breaks decisively, its role flips:

  • Broken Resistance becomes the new Support
  • Broken Support becomes the new Resistance
Dynamic S&R — The Channel

When support and resistance slope with the trend, drawn along the higher lows and higher highs, you get a trading channel — buy the floor, sell the ceiling.

Block IV · Trend Tracking

The Moving Average & Bollinger Bands

The baseline of every trend-following system — and its 2σ envelope

Simple Moving Average

SMA = Σ closesN / N

The rule: buy when price crosses above the upward-sloping SMA or bounces off it as support; sell when it breaks below.

Fatal limit: lag. Short windows produce whipsaws; long windows react too slowly to sudden crashes.

Bollinger Bands

Upper/Lower = SMA20 ± 2σ

The rule: 95% of historical price movement should occur inside the bands. When price closes back inside from below, that confirms mean reversion.

Fatal limit: in strong trends price can ride the band, sliding along the boundary for extended stretches.

Block IV · Oscillators

Measuring Momentum — When Speed Tells the Story

Like a ball thrown in the air — you can tell it’s about to fall when its rise slows

Momentum Indicator

M = Closet − Closet−N

The rule: enter when the indicator crosses above the zero line; exit when it crosses below.

Fatal limit: noise. Rapid-fire signals chop across zero, leading to overtrading.

Relative Strength Index (RSI)

RSI = 100 − 100 / (1 + RS)

The rule: > 70 is overbought, < 30 is oversold. The strongest signal is divergence: price hits a lower low but RSI hits a higher low.

Fatal limit: in strong trends, RSI can stay mathematically overbought (or oversold) for long stretches before mean-reverting.

Block IV · The Macro Layer

Polling the Crowd Before You Trade the Chart

The contrarian’s thermometer — sentiment extremes mark market turning points

Sentiment

Put/Call Ratio · VIX

Baseline: < 1.0 — investors are naturally bullish. A spike > 1.0 signals excessive, panic-level bearishness — which, contrarians argue, usually marks a market bottom.

Companion metrics: VIX (volatility index), consumer sentiment polls, historical P/E ratios.

Flow of Funds

TRIN (Arms Index)

Baseline: < 1.0 — money is aggressively flowing into advancing stocks. A massive spike confirms either a final surge before a reversal or a broad-based rally that’s secretly weakening beneath the surface.

Companion metrics: On-Balance Volume, Chaikin Money Flow, Commitment of Traders report.

How to use the macro layer
Macro indicators are not for individual stock triggers. They set the backdrop — confirming when a broad rally is real (volume agrees) or when the crowd is priced for disappointment (sentiment extremes).
