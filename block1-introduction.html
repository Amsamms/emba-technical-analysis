What is Technical Analysis?

Technical Analysis is the study of past market data — primarily price and volume — to forecast future price movements.

Who Uses It?

  • Day Traders — short-term momentum plays
  • Hedge Funds — quantitative strategies
  • Retail Investors — entry/exit timing
  • Algorithmic Systems — automated pattern detection
  • Investment Banks — risk management & positioning
"The market is a barometer, not a thermometer. It tells you what is going to happen, not what has already happened." — Charles Dow, founder of Dow Jones & Co.

Key Distinction

TA focuses on what the market is doing (price action), not why it is doing it (fundamentals).

KEY POINTS:
  • TA is the study of price and volume data to forecast future movements
  • Used by everyone from retail traders to billion-dollar hedge funds
  • Charles Dow — the father of modern TA — saw the market as forward-looking
SAY: "Technical Analysis is essentially about reading the market's own story through price and volume. Unlike Fundamental Analysis, which digs into financial statements and earnings, TA looks at what the market is actually doing. And as Charles Dow said, the market acts as a barometer — it is forward-looking."

A Brief History of Technical Analysis

Munehisa Homma (1700s)

Japanese rice trader who invented candlestick charts — still the most popular chart type today.

Charles Dow (1890s)

Created the Dow Jones Industrial Average and laid the groundwork for modern TA with Dow Theory.

Edwards & Magee (1948)

Published "Technical Analysis of Stock Trends" — the definitive textbook still in use.

KEY POINTS:
  • TA has roots going back 300+ years to Japanese rice trading
  • Homma's candlestick charts are still the gold standard
  • Charles Dow formalized TA theory in the 1890s
  • Computers in the 1960s, online trading in the 1990s, and AI in the 2020s each transformed the field
SAY: "Technical Analysis has a rich history spanning over 300 years. It began with Munehisa Homma, a Japanese rice trader who developed candlestick charts in the 1700s — and remarkably, these are still the most popular chart type today. Then Charles Dow formalized the theory in the 1890s. The real acceleration came with computers in the 1960s, online trading in the 1990s, and now AI is taking it to a whole new level."

The Core Question

"Where is the price
going next?"

TA Answers: WHEN

When to buy or sell.
Timing entries and exits based on price signals.

TA Does NOT Answer: WHAT

What to buy based on value.
That is the domain of Fundamental Analysis.

Think of it this way: FA tells you which stocks are undervalued. TA tells you the right moment to act on that information.

KEY POINTS:
  • TA is fundamentally about predicting future price direction
  • It answers WHEN to buy/sell, not WHAT to buy
  • FA and TA serve complementary purposes
SAY: "At its heart, Technical Analysis asks one simple question: where is the price going next? But here is the crucial distinction — TA tells you WHEN to act. It does not tell you WHAT to buy based on a company's intrinsic value. That is why many professional traders use both approaches together. FA picks the stocks, TA picks the timing."

Connection to Our Course

The EMH Challenge

The Efficient Market Hypothesis (Chapter 9) argues that all available information is already reflected in stock prices — making it impossible to consistently beat the market using TA.

Weak-form EMH: Past prices contain no useful predictive information.

The Practitioner's Response

Yet billions of dollars in trading profits are generated annually by TA-based strategies. Are markets truly efficient, or do behavioral biases create exploitable patterns?

Course Context

  • Chapter 9: Efficient Market Hypothesis — the academic case against TA
  • Chapter 12: Behavioral Finance — why markets may NOT be efficient
  • Our presentation: The practitioner's view — does TA work despite EMH?

Our Goal: Present TA objectively — its tools, evidence, and limitations — so you can form your own judgment on whether markets are truly efficient.

KEY POINTS:
