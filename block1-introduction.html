What is Technical Analysis?

Technical Analysis is the study of past market data — primarily price and volume — to forecast future price movements.

Who Uses It?

  • Day Traders — short-term momentum plays
  • Hedge Funds — quantitative strategies
  • Retail Investors — entry/exit timing
  • Algorithmic Systems — automated pattern detection
  • Investment Banks — risk management & positioning
"The market is a barometer, not a thermometer. It tells you what is going to happen, not what has already happened." — Charles Dow, founder of Dow Jones & Co.

Key Distinction

TA focuses on what the market is doing (price action), not why it is doing it (fundamentals).

KEY POINTS:
  • TA is the study of price and volume data to forecast future movements
  • Used by everyone from retail traders to billion-dollar hedge funds
  • Charles Dow — the father of modern TA — saw the market as forward-looking
SAY: "Technical Analysis is essentially about reading the market's own story through price and volume. Unlike Fundamental Analysis, which digs into financial statements and earnings, TA looks at what the market is actually doing. And as Charles Dow said, the market acts as a barometer — it is forward-looking."

The Ancient Roots of Market Analysis

Before Charts Existed — Humans Were Already Reading Markets

Babylonian Traders (~2000 BC)

Clay tablets from ancient Mesopotamia record commodity prices for grain, wool, and sesame over time. Merchants tracked price fluctuations to time their trades — the earliest known form of market data analysis.

4,000 years before computers, traders were already looking for patterns.

Dutch Tulip Mania (1636-37)

The first documented speculative bubble. Tulip bulb prices rose 20x in months, then crashed 99%. Observers noted the classic pattern: euphoria → mania → panic → crash. This boom-bust cycle would repeat throughout history.

The pattern of human greed and fear — TA's foundation.

Joseph de la Vega (1688)

Wrote "Confusion de Confusiones" — the first book describing stock exchange mechanics at the Amsterdam Stock Exchange. He documented market manipulation, speculation tactics, and observed that prices move in predictable emotional cycles.

The first written analysis of market psychology.

Key Insight: Long before any formal theory, humans intuitively understood that markets move in patterns driven by human psychology — greed, fear, and herd behavior. This is the foundation that all of Technical Analysis is built upon.

KEY POINTS:
  • Market analysis is NOT a modern invention — it goes back 4,000 years
  • Babylonian clay tablets are the earliest known "price charts"
  • Dutch Tulip Mania (1636) showed the first documented boom-bust cycle
  • De la Vega's 1688 book is the first written work on market psychology
SAY: "The story of Technical Analysis begins much earlier than most people realize. Four thousand years ago, Babylonian merchants were already recording commodity prices on clay tablets, looking for patterns to time their trades. In 1636, the Dutch Tulip Mania gave us the first documented speculative bubble — prices rose 20 times then crashed 99%. And in 1688, Joseph de la Vega wrote the first book describing how stock exchanges work, noting that prices move in predictable emotional cycles. The core insight? Markets are driven by human psychology — and psychology creates patterns."

Munehisa Homma & The Birth of Candlesticks

Dojima Rice Exchange, Osaka, Japan — 1700s

The "God of Markets"

Munehisa Homma (1724–1803) was a Japanese rice trader from Sakata who became the most successful trader of his era. He made the equivalent of $10 billion in today's money trading rice futures at the Dojima Rice Exchange in Osaka.

Homma did not just trade — he invented a visual language for reading markets. His candlestick charts encoded four data points (open, high, low, close) into a single visual element, making price action instantly readable.

Sakata's Five Methods

Homma developed five strategic rules for reading market conditions, known as the Sakata Rules. They identified five key market states:

  • San-zan (Three Mountains) — triple top reversal
  • San-sen (Three Rivers) — triple bottom reversal
  • San-ku (Three Gaps) — exhaustion signals
  • San-pei (Three Soldiers) — continuation strength
  • San-pou (Three Methods) — rest then continuation
"When all are bearish, there is cause for prices to rise. When all are bullish, there is cause for prices to fall." — Munehisa Homma, "The Fountain of Gold" (c. 1755)

Why Candlesticks Still Dominate

  • Visual clarity — instantly shows bullish vs bearish sentiment
  • Pattern recognition — Doji, Hammer, Engulfing, Morning Star
  • Psychology encoded — each candle tells a story of buyer/seller battle
  • Universal — works for stocks, forex, crypto, commodities

Homma's key insight: markets are not driven by logic alone — they are driven by the emotions of their participants.

Fun Fact: Homma's candlestick method remained a Japanese secret for nearly 200 years until Steve Nison introduced it to the West in 1991.

KEY POINTS:
  • Homma made the equivalent of $10 billion trading rice in 18th century Japan
  • He invented candlestick charts — STILL the most popular chart type worldwide
  • His Sakata Rules identified 5 market states — many are still used today (e.g., Three Soldiers)
  • His core philosophy: market emotion (greed and fear) is the key driver of prices
  • Candlesticks remained a Japanese secret until Steve Nison published his book in 1991
SAY: "Now we come to the true father of Technical Analysis — Munehisa Homma, a Japanese rice trader in the 1700s. Homma was extraordinary. Trading at the Dojima Rice Exchange in Osaka, he amassed a fortune equivalent to 10 billion dollars today. But his real legacy is the candlestick chart — a visual system that encodes four data points into one symbol. He also developed the Sakata Rules — five patterns for reading market conditions. Many of these patterns, like Three Soldiers and Three Mountains, are still used by traders today, 270 years later. And here is what is remarkable — his candlestick method remained a Japanese secret for nearly 200 years. The West only learned about it in 1991."

Charles Dow & The Birth of Modern TA

Wall Street, 1880s–1900s — The Framework That Built an Industry

The Man Behind the Index

Charles Henry Dow (1851–1902) was a journalist and co-founder of Dow Jones & Company (1882) with Edward Jones and Charles Bergstresser. He founded the Wall Street Journal and created the Dow Jones Industrial Average (1896) — originally tracking just 12 industrial stocks.

Dow never wrote a book or published a formal theory. His ideas were expressed through a series of 255 editorials in the Wall Street Journal between 1899 and 1902. After his death, William Hamilton and Robert Rhea compiled these editorials into what we now call Dow Theory.

"The market is a barometer, not a thermometer. It tells you what is going to happen, not what has already happened." — Charles Dow, Wall Street Journal editorial

The Six Tenets of Dow Theory

  1. The market discounts everything — all known information is already reflected in prices
  2. Three types of trends exist — primary (months–years), secondary (weeks–months), minor (days–weeks)
  3. Primary trends have three phases — accumulation, public participation, distribution
  4. Indices must confirm each other — Industrial and Transportation averages must move in the same direction
  5. Volume confirms the trend — volume should increase in the direction of the primary trend
  6. Trends persist until clear reversal — a trend is in effect until definitive signals prove it has ended

Legacy: Every modern TA concept — trend following, support/resistance, volume analysis, market phases — traces back to these six tenets. Dow Theory is the DNA of Technical Analysis.

KEY POINTS:
  • Charles Dow co-founded Dow Jones & Company (1882) and the Wall Street Journal
  • Created the DJIA in 1896 — originally just 12 stocks
  • He never wrote a formal theory — his ideas come from 255 WSJ editorials (1899-1902)
  • William Hamilton and Robert Rhea later compiled these into "Dow Theory"
  • The 6 tenets of Dow Theory form the foundation of ALL modern TA
  • Concepts like "the market discounts everything" and "trends have 3 phases" are still core principles
SAY: "If Homma is the father of candlestick charting, Charles Dow is the father of modern Technical Analysis as a whole. Dow co-founded Dow Jones and Company in 1882, created the Wall Street Journal, and in 1896 launched the Dow Jones Industrial Average — originally tracking just 12 stocks. Here is what makes Dow fascinating — he never wrote a book. His entire theory comes from 255 editorials he wrote in the Wall Street Journal. After his death in 1902, others compiled his ideas into what we now call Dow Theory. And these six tenets — the market discounts everything, trends have three phases, volume confirms the trend — these are STILL the foundation of everything we do in TA today."

The Early Pioneers (1900s–1940s)

Three Visionaries Who Shaped How We Read Markets

Richard Wyckoff (1873–1934)

The Tape Reader

Started as a stock runner at age 15 on Wall Street. Developed the Wyckoff Method — a systematic approach to analyzing market structure through price action and volume.

  • Introduced the concept of the "Composite Operator" — smart money that moves markets
  • Pioneered volume spread analysis (VSA)
  • Founded the Magazine of Wall Street (1907)
  • His method identifies 4 market phases: accumulation, markup, distribution, markdown

Still Used: Wyckoff Method is taught at CMT exams and used by institutional traders today.

W.D. Gann (1878–1955)

The Geometric Mystic

One of the most controversial figures in TA. Gann believed markets follow geometric and mathematical laws rooted in natural cycles.

  • Gann Angles — geometric trendlines (1x1, 2x1) for support/resistance
  • Square of Nine — a mathematical tool for price/time projections
  • Time cycles — predicted market turns based on astronomical and calendar cycles
  • Reportedly turned $130 into $12,000 in 30 trading days (1909, verified by Ticker Magazine)

Controversy: Gann's methods mix geometry, astrology, and numerology. Debated but still followed by a devoted community.

R.N. Elliott (1871–1948)

The Wave Theorist

A retired accountant who, while recovering from illness, studied 75 years of market data and discovered that markets move in repetitive wave patterns.

  • Published "The Wave Principle" (1938)
  • Markets move in 5 impulse waves + 3 corrective waves = 8-wave cycle
  • Waves follow Fibonacci ratios (1.618, 0.618, 0.382)
  • The theory applies across all timeframes — from minutes to decades (fractal nature)

Impact: Elliott Wave Theory remains one of the most widely discussed TA frameworks. Fibonacci retracements are standard on every trading platform.

KEY POINTS:
  • Wyckoff: volume-based analysis, "Composite Operator" (smart money), 4 market phases — still used by institutions
  • Gann: geometric/mathematical approach, controversial but influential — Gann angles still on charting platforms
  • Elliott: wave theory with Fibonacci ratios — one of the most discussed TA frameworks ever
  • Each pioneer added a different lens: Wyckoff (volume), Gann (geometry), Elliott (fractals)
SAY: "The early 1900s produced three remarkable pioneers. Richard Wyckoff was a tape reader on Wall Street who developed a method for following smart money through volume analysis — his framework is still taught in professional certification exams today. W.D. Gann was the most controversial — he believed markets follow geometric and mathematical laws, and reportedly turned 130 dollars into 12,000 in just 30 days, verified by a magazine in 1909. And Ralph Nelson Elliott, a retired accountant, spent years studying market data and discovered that prices move in repetitive wave patterns based on Fibonacci ratios. Each of these men added a different dimension to TA — volume, geometry, and fractals."

The Formalization Era (1948–1970s)

From Art to Discipline — TA Gets Its Bible

"Technical Analysis of Stock Trends" (1948)

Robert Edwards and John Magee published what is universally known as the "Bible of Technical Analysis" — the first comprehensive, systematic catalog of chart patterns and their predictive significance.

  • Robert Edwards was the brother-in-law of Richard Schabacker (1902–1938), an earlier pioneer who first classified chart patterns but died young
  • Edwards inherited Schabacker's research and partnered with John Magee to expand and formalize it
  • The book cataloged head & shoulders, double tops/bottoms, triangles, flags, wedges — patterns still used in every trading platform today
  • Now in its 11th edition — continuously updated for 76 years
"Charts reflect human behavior, and human behavior does not change." — John Magee

The Rise of the Chartist Profession

For the first time, TA became a recognized discipline on Wall Street:

  • 1960s: Brokerage firms began hiring dedicated "chartists" and technical analysts
  • 1969: The Market Technicians Association (MTA) was founded — the first professional body for technical analysts
  • 1970s: The Chartered Market Technician (CMT) designation was introduced — TA's equivalent of the CFA for fundamental analysis
  • Point & Figure charting became a standard Wall Street methodology alongside bar charts

Academic Pushback Begins

In 1965, Eugene Fama published his Efficient Market Hypothesis (EMH) — arguing that if markets are efficient, TA cannot consistently work. This sparked a debate between academics and practitioners that continues to this day.

We will explore this debate in detail in Block 5.

KEY POINTS:
  • Edwards & Magee (1948) published the "Bible of TA" — now in its 11th edition, 76 years later
  • Edwards inherited research from Schabacker, who first classified chart patterns but died at age 36
  • The 1960s-70s saw TA become a legitimate profession: dedicated chartist roles, MTA founded (1969), CMT designation
  • Eugene Fama's EMH (1965) challenged TA's validity from the academic side — this debate is STILL ongoing
SAY: "By 1948, enough knowledge had accumulated for Edwards and Magee to publish what became known as the Bible of Technical Analysis. This was the first time anyone systematically cataloged all the chart patterns — head and shoulders, double tops, triangles — that we still use today. The book is now in its 11th edition, 76 years later. This era also saw TA become a real profession. The Market Technicians Association was founded in 1969, and the CMT designation gave technical analysts their own professional certification, similar to the CFA. But this era also saw the beginning of the academic challenge — in 1965, Eugene Fama published the Efficient Market Hypothesis, arguing that TA cannot consistently beat the market. That debate continues today, and we will explore it in Block 5."

From Computers to AI (1970s–Present)

The Acceleration That Transformed TA Forever

The Indicator Revolution (1970s–80s)

Computers enabled rapid calculation of complex mathematical indicators for the first time:

  • J. Welles Wilder Jr. (1978) — invented RSI, ATR, Parabolic SAR, ADX in one landmark book: "New Concepts in Technical Trading Systems"
  • Gerald Appel (1979) — created MACD (Moving Average Convergence Divergence)
  • John Bollinger (1983) — developed Bollinger Bands using standard deviation

One man — Wilder — invented 4 of the most used indicators in a single book.

The Digital Democratization (1990s–2000s)

TA went from Wall Street privilege to global accessibility:

  • Steve Nison (1991) — published "Japanese Candlestick Charting Techniques," bringing Homma's 200-year-old method to the Western world
  • Online brokers (E*Trade, Schwab) — made trading accessible to everyone
  • Day trading boom — millions of retail traders entered markets
  • Bloomberg Terminal — institutionalized TA with built-in tools

The AI & Algo Era (2010s–Now)

Machines now dominate market analysis and execution:

  • 70%+ of trading is now algorithmic — most using TA signals
  • Machine learning identifies patterns humans cannot see
  • High-frequency trading (HFT) executes in microseconds
  • TradingView — 60M+ users democratize advanced TA
  • AI chatbots explain TA concepts to beginners in real-time

The Full Circle: From Homma hand-drawing candles on rice paper in 1750s Japan → to AI algorithms processing millions of candlesticks per second in 2026. The tools changed beyond recognition, but the core premise remains: price patterns driven by human psychology repeat — and they can be exploited.

KEY POINTS:
  • 1970s-80s: Wilder single-handedly invented RSI, ATR, Parabolic SAR, and ADX in ONE book (1978)
  • Appel created MACD (1979), Bollinger created Bollinger Bands (1983)
  • 1991: Steve Nison finally brought Japanese candlesticks to the West — 200 years after Homma
  • 1990s: Online trading democratized TA — no longer a Wall Street exclusive
  • Today: 70%+ of trading is algorithmic, AI/ML finding patterns beyond human capability
  • Full circle: same psychology-based premise, infinitely more powerful tools
SAY: "The computer revolution of the 1970s was a turning point. In 1978, one man — J. Welles Wilder — published a single book that introduced RSI, ATR, Parabolic SAR, and ADX — four indicators that are on literally every trading platform today. Then in 1991, Steve Nison finally introduced Japanese candlestick charts to the West, 200 years after Homma invented them. The 1990s brought online trading, democratizing TA for millions. And today, over 70 percent of all trading is algorithmic, with AI and machine learning finding patterns beyond human capability. Think about the full circle — from hand-drawn candles on rice paper in 1750s Japan to AI processing millions of candlesticks per second. The tools are unrecognizable, but the core idea remains: human psychology creates repeating patterns, and those patterns can be exploited."

The Core Question

"Where is the price
going next?"

TA Answers: WHEN

When to buy or sell.
Timing entries and exits based on price signals.

TA Does NOT Answer: WHAT

What to buy based on value.
That is the domain of Fundamental Analysis.

Think of it this way: FA tells you which stocks are undervalued. TA tells you the right moment to act on that information.

KEY POINTS:
  • TA is fundamentally about predicting future price direction
  • It answers WHEN to buy/sell, not WHAT to buy
  • FA and TA serve complementary purposes
SAY: "At its heart, Technical Analysis asks one simple question: where is the price going next? But here is the crucial distinction — TA tells you WHEN to act. It does not tell you WHAT to buy based on a company's intrinsic value. That is why many professional traders use both approaches together. FA picks the stocks, TA picks the timing."
